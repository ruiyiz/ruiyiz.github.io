Solutions to UVIMCO Screening Questions

Solutions to UVIMCO Screening Questions

Ruiyi Zhang, CFA (me@ruiyizhang.com)

2015-09-26

This document presents my solutions to UVIMCO’s job screening questions. It is intended as a supporting document for my job application to the Risk Investment Associate position (Req ID: 1015) at UVIMCO.

MATLAB implementations are provided to aid the presentation wherever appropriate. In order to show the algorithms in their most organic form, MATLAB built-in functions are intentionally avoided if at all possible.

For space saving reasons, not all MATLAB code are listed herein. The complete source code, datasets, as well as the R Markdown file that typesets this document are all made available online (download link).

Question 1

Manager A is a Long-only equity manager. We have their most recent 10yr monthly returns history as well as their benchmark returns, e.g. the S&P 500:

  1. What do you do to evaluate manager A’s performance? What are some measurements you would look at and what are the formula and procedure to calculate them?
  2. How do we estimate A’s risk given we only have A’s return data?
  3. How would you estimate A’s “active” risk?

ANSWER:

a) To evaluate a manager’s performance, I would compile measurements that can be grouped into roughly three buckets – cumulative performance, return statistics, and risk-adjusted returns. These metrics are then evaluated on both an absolute basis and a comparative basis against a benchmark.

The following introduces the metrics, discusses the interpretations, and outlines the formulas and processes required to calculate them. Wherever appropriate, MATLAB code are also presented with sample results. The sample data used in this answer are the monthly returns of Fidelity Magellan Fund (symbol: FMAGX), a long-only Large Growth fund. The benchmark is the monthly return series of S&P 500 Total Return Index (symbol: SP500TR).

load AssetReturn
fts = extfield(MonthlyReturn, {'FMAGX', 'SP500TR'});

In formulas that follow, let \(R = \left\{ r_t: t \in T \right\}\) be the return time series, and \(n\) be the number of observations.

  1. Cumulative Performance

    The cumulative return answer the question – “what return on investment would I expect if I invested in this fund T years ago?” This is the return you would earn on an initial investment with no subsequent deposits or withdraws. The cumulative return between any arbitrary times \(t_1\) and \(t_2\) is defined as:

    \[r_{C, t_1, t_2} = \prod_{i=t_1}^{t_2} (1 + r_i) - 1\]

    As the choice of measurement periods could make for large differences in the results even for the same manager, it is important to look at cumulative returns over multiple measurement periods. These periods could be trailing X-years or rolling window of Y-year duration.

    % NOTE: source file of function plotcumrets is separately attached.
subplot(1, 2, 1);
plotcumrets(fts, 10);
subplot(1, 2, 2);
plotcumrets(fts, 5);

  2. Return Statistics

    To gauge the level, volatility, and the dispersion of a manager’s returns over time, statistical measurements can be calculated for the manager’s returns. These metrics include, but not limited to:

    • Minimum and Maximum: the lowest and highest monthly returns, respectively, achieved over the measurement period.
    • Quartiles (1st quartile, median, 3rd quartile): measures the dispersion of returns.
    • Moments and Central Moments of the return distribution

      • Mean (1st moment): the return with the highest probability of occurring.

        \[\mu = \operatorname{E}[R] = \frac{1}{n} \displaystyle\sum_{T} r_t\]

      • Variance (2nd central moment): measures the variability of returns.The postive square root of variance \(\sigma^2\) is the standard deviation (\(\sigma\)) of the return distribution.

        \[\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n} \displaystyle\sum_{T} (r_t - \bar{r})^2\]

      • Skewness (normalized 3rd central moment): asset returns unlikely follow a Normal distribution. Skewness measures the return distribution’s deformation from a Normal one. A negative skewness indicates that there are more extreme negative returns in the left tail, and vice versa.

        \[\mathrm{Skewness} = \frac{1}{n} \displaystyle\sum_{T} \left( \frac{r_t - \bar{r}}{\sigma} \right) ^3\]

      • Kurtosis (normalized 4th central moment): measures the “peakiness” of a distribution. Compared to a Normal distribution, which has a kurtosis of 3, a distribution would exhibit more “peakiness” around the mean and heavier tails if its kurtosis is greater than 3. Likewise, a kurtosis less than 3 indicates a shallower peak and leaner tails of a distribution.

        \[\mathrm{Kurtosis} = \frac{1}{n} \displaystyle\sum_{T} \left( \frac{r_t - \bar{r}}{\sigma} \right) ^4\]

    • Geometric Mean

      \[r_G = \left(\prod_{T} (1 + r_t)\right)^{\frac{f}{n}} - 1\]

      where \(f\) is the number of subperiods in a period over which the geometric mean is calculated. For instance, an \(f\) of 12 applied to monthly returns would result in an annualized geometric mean.

    Again, all these metrics can be calculated and compared over various trailing and rolling periods to shed more light on how the manager’s performance characteristics have evolved over time.

  3. Risk-Adjusted Returns

    Recognizing that, for the same return, investors would prefer an investment vehicle that is less risky, i.e. investors are risk averse, Risk-Adjusted return measurements attempt to standardize returns on a risk-aware basis so that investment opportunities with very different risk characteristics can be compared and ranked with as little bias as possible. They are generally either in a ratio form, or in a return form with adjustments based on some risk measures.

    The most commonly cited risk-adjusted return ratio is perhaps the Sharpe ratio, which is a ratio between the investment’s return and risk, with the excess return over the risk-free return in the numerator, and the standard deviation of the investment’s returns as the denominator. The higher the ratio, the higher the return the investment earned per unit of risk it undertook.

    \[\mathrm{SR} = \frac{r-r_f}{\sigma}\]

    The formula is meant to be applied to annualized returns and risks. Annualized returns can be calculated using the Geometric Mean formula described above for annual frequency, whereas the annualized risk can be calculated by multiplying the monthly standard deviation by \(\sqrt{12}\).

    Of the risk-adjusted return variants, \(M^2\) is a popular choice and it is closely related to the Sharpe ratio.

    \[M^2 = r + \mathrm{SR} (\sigma_B - \sigma)\]

    Here \(\sigma_B\) is the annualized standard deviation of the benchmark.

b) There are seemingly endless ways to slice and dice the returns to gauge an investment vehicle’s risks, even with only a monthly return series provided. It would be futile to enumerate them in this short answer. Instead, I would try to give a rundown of the key categories of risk measurements – namely volatility risks, downside risks, and tail risks – and highlight one or more representative ones out of each category.

  1. Volatility Risks

    Volatility risk measurements use volatilities as the proxy for risk. This is almost certainly the most recognized category of risk measurements for their prominent roles in many monumental Financial Economics theories, including the Modern Portfolio Theory and Sharpe ratio.

    Critics of this type of risk measurements complain, and rightly so, that they tend to give equal weighting to both upward and downward movements and don’t distinguish the “good” volatilities from the “bad”. Despite the widely acknowledged shortcomings, these metrics have still been the mainstay of the lexicon of modern investment management.

    • Mean Absolute Deviation: measures the variability of the return series. To calculate, the sum of the absolute different between returns and the mean return is divided by the number of observations. The argument in favor of MAD over standard deviation is that it is less affected by extreme outliers, which happen a lot in financial contexts (considered extreme by the standard of Normal distributions at least).

      \[\mathrm{MAD} = \frac{1}{n} \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} |r_i - \bar{r}|\]

    • Standard Deviation: also measures the variability of the returns. However, it is defined as the positive square root of variance, which is the sum of squared differences between returns and the mean return divided by the number of observations.

      \[\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} (r_i - \bar{r})^2}\]

  2. Downside Risks

    For most investors who take directional risks, the true exposure is usually only on the downside. Downside risk measurements, unlike volatility risks, intentionally differentiate upside and downside risks, and only concern the downside.

    • Semi-Deviation: measures the variability of underperformance below a minimum target return, which is typically the risk-free return or the benchmark return. The formula is similar to the “standard” standard deviation formula except all positive returns are replaced by zero.

      \[\sigma_D = \sqrt{\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \operatorname{min} [(r_i - r_T), 0]^2}{n}}\]

      where \(r_T\) is the minimum target return.

    • Maximum Drawdown: measures the maximum severity of a sustained losing streak. It is the maximum potential loss an investor of the investment vehicle could sustain by buying at the absolute high point (the peak) and selling at the absolute low (the trough) over a specific measurement period.

      Given a cumulative return series \(r_{C}\), the drawdown at any time \(T\) is the greater of 1) the maximum of all cumulative returns preceded \(T\) minus the return at \(T\), and 2) zero.

      \[\mathrm{DD}(T) = \operatorname{max} \left\{ 0, \underset{t \in (0, T)}{\operatorname{max}} r_{C}(t) - r_{C}(T) \right\}\]

      The maximum drawdown up to time \(T\) is simply the maximum of the drawdowns between time \(0\) and \(T\).

  3. Tail Risks

    Tail risks, as the name suggests, studies investment consequences arising from extreme tail events. It attempts to answer the question – “what is the potential loss a(n) investment/portfolio/firm would expect to sustain if a 1-in-200 event happens over the next 1 year?” The key parameters in this question are:

    1. the risk characteristics of the subject (FMAGX, for example),
    2. the confidence level (1-in-200 event, or 99.5% confidence level in the example), and
    3. the risk horizon (the next 1 year).

    Tail risks are probabilistic in nature, and the key is the construction of a credible ex ante probability distribution.

    Among the litany of tail risk measurements, Value-at-Risk is indisputably the most widely used of them all. Popularized by RiskMetrics, it has enjoyed enduring popularity in literatures, deployed on trading floors the world over, and even found its way into prominent regulatory frameworks (Basel II & Solvency II).

    VaR is defined as the smallest amount \(l\) such that there is no more chance than \((1-\alpha)\) that the loss \(L\) would exceeds \(l\). Formally,

    \[\mathrm{VaR}_{\alpha}(L) = \operatorname{inf}\left\{ l \in \mathbb{R} : P(L > l) \le 1 - \alpha \right\}\]

    In practice, VaR is typically calculated in three different methods.

    • Variance-Covariance: with the strong assumption of Normality of the return distribution, VaR at confidence level \(\alpha\) can be calculated as:

      \[\mathrm{VaR} = \bar{r} - \Phi^{-1}(\alpha) \times \sigma\]

      where \(\Phi^{-1}\) is the inverse cumulative distribution function of standard Normal distribution.
    • Historical Simulation: with the assumption that the past is a good representation of the future, actual historical returns are rank ordered to approximate the ex ante distribution of future returns. Procedurally, historical returns are rank ordered from the worst to best, and VaR at confidence level \(\alpha\) is simply determined at the \((1-\alpha)\)th percentile.

    • Monte-Carlo Simulation: similar to historical simulation, but with the historical return data replaced by models that are deemed to be good representations of future returns.

c) Active Risk is the risk caused by holdings deviate from those of the benchmark in an effort to outperform the benchmark. It can be measured as tracking error, which is the variability of a manager’s excess return over a benchmark. This excess return is often called active premium. The formula for calculating tracking error is:

\[\mathrm{TE} = \sqrt{\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}(a_i - \bar{a})^2}{n}}\]

Note the calculation is the same as the standard deviation, except active premium \(a\) takes the place of the usual returns.

Question 2

Suppose, instead, that Manager A is a Long-Short manager. In general, is there anything you can say about his net exposure and his beta to the benchmark?

ANSWER:

A long-short strategy aims to maximize the manager’s stock selection skills by not only acting on her bullish insights on some stocks, but also taking advantage of her bearish opinions on other stocks. The basics are the manager takes long positions in stocks expected to rise in price and simultaneously short-selling stocks she may think as overvalued. The strategy is often motivated to minimize market exposure in general, but that does not need to be the case. Therefore, the net exposure depends on the actual construction of the long-short portfolio.

In a market-neutral setting, the long and short positions are balanced such that the systematic risk is entirely neutralized. In such a case, the portfolio is in theory free of systematic risk, and only exposed to adverse changes in spreads between the long and short positions. With no exposure to the market, the strategy’s beta to the benchmark would be 0.

Equitized long-short strategy is another popular variant. Under this setting, in addition to exercising her stock selection skills, the manager may also desire the full market exposure through derivatives (e.g. index futures). The rationale is that the market tends to trend up in general. The equitized strategy would be exposed to the systematic risk in its entirety, in addition to the spread risk inherent in the long-short positions. The beta of a equitized strategy is approximately 1.

In fact, the manager could elect to take on any amount of systematic risk by varying the derivatives exposures, or the manager may take on unbalanced long-short positions. Consequently, the net exposure and beta must be analyzed on a case-by-case basis for non-standard long-short strategies.

Remarks:

  • S&P 500 Index contains only long positions and may not be the most appropriate benchmark for a long-short manager.
  • It is assumed the beta coefficients of the stocks are stationary over time. If not, the long-short positions that started out as market-neutral may become directional over time, therefore leave the portfolio exposured to the systematic risk.

Question 3

A manager has exposure to the style factors, “value” and “size” with betas equal to -0.8 and 0.6 respectively and exposure to the market (S&P 500) return with beta 1.2.

  1. What can you say about this manager’s style?
  2. Suppose you didn’t want the exposure to the market. Describe a strategy that could hedge that exposure away in futures.

ANSWER:

a) Assume the “value” and “size” factors are defined in a similar fashion as the Fama-French model. i.e.:

  • “value”: the return of the value portfolio with low P/E ratios minus that of the growth portfolio with high P/E ratios.
  • “size”: the return of the small market-cap portfolio minus that of the large market-cap portfolio.

The negative reading on the “value” beta suggests the manager underweights value stocks and overweights growth stocks. The positive value of the “size” beta signals an overweighting of small cap stocks. All told, the manager’s style can be best characterized as Small Growth.

b) To completely neutralize the market factor exposure, a short position in S&P 500 Index futures could be taken, with the contract size determined by the following formula, assuming fractional contracts are possible.

\[\mathrm{NumOfContracts} = \frac{\mathrm{Notional_{portfolio}} \times -\beta_{Market}} {\mathrm{Notional_{futures}}} = \frac{\mathrm{Notional_{portfolio}} \times -\beta_{Market}} {\mathrm{Price_{futures}} \times \mathrm{Multiplier}}\]

A negative sign of \(\mathrm{NumOfContracts}\) would suggest a short position should be taken. The multipliers for regular S&P 500 futures contracts and the E-mini ones are 250 and 50, respectively.

Question 4

Suppose you want to regress a manager’s returns on the three Fama-French factors. Write down the code to perform a multivariate linear regression analysis in R. Repeat in Matlab. Repeat in Excel.

ANSWER:

Fama-French 3-factor model:

\[r = \alpha + r_f + \beta_m (r_m - r_f) + \beta_s r_s + \beta_v r_v\]

where \(r_f\) is the risk-free rate, \(r_m\) is the return on the market portfolio, \(r_s\) is the average differential of historical returns on small-cap stocks over large-cap stocks, and \(r_v\) is the average differential of historical returns on value stock with low Price-to-Book ratio over growth stocks with high Price-to-Book ratio (note that Fama-French uses B/P ratio in the construction of the HML factor, so High-minus-Low in the context of Fama-French is the same as the differential of value over growth).

The regression model is specified as:

\[(r_t - r_{ft}) = \alpha + \beta_m (r_{mt} - r_{ft}) + \beta_s r_{st} + \beta_v r_{vt} + \epsilon_t\]

MATLAB Solution

In the following analysis, we regress 20-year worth of monthly returns of Fidelity Magellan Fund against the Fama-French factors. The factors are retrieved from Kenneth French’s website.

trailingperiod = '30-JUL-1995::30-JUN-2015';

% Load Fama-French 3-factor model factors

Question 5

Let \(\mathbf{X}\) be a \(T \times k\) matrix of \(k\) asset returns over time \(T\).

  1. How would you calculate the covariance matrix \(\mathbf{V}\)?
  2. Prove that \(\mathbf{S} \mathbf{C} \mathbf{S} = \mathbf{V}\) where \(\mathbf{S}\) is a diagonal matrix of standard deviations and \(\mathbf{C}\) is a \(k \times k\) correlation matrix.
  3. True/False: the first principal component of \(\mathbf{V}\) is a portfolio.

ANSWER:

a) For matrix \(\mathbf{X}\) of size \(T \times k\), the covariance matrix is a square matrix defined as [1]:

\[\mathbf{V} = \begin{pmatrix} v_{1,1} & v_{1,2} & \cdots & v_{1,k} \\ v_{2,1} & v_{2,2} & \cdots & v_{2,k} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ v_{k,1} & v_{k,2} & \cdots & v_{k,k} \end{pmatrix}\]

where \(v_{i,j}\)’s are pairwise covariances between random variables \(X_i\) and \(X_j\), and are defined as [2]:

\[v_{i,j} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{i,p} - \bar{X_i})(x_{j,p} - \bar{X_j})} {(n-1)}\]

where \(\bar{X_{i}}\) is the expected value of RV \(X_i\), and \(n\) is the number of observations.

Combine [1] and [2], and extract the denominator \((n-1)\) from the matrix, we have [3]:

\[\mathbf{V} = \begin{pmatrix} \sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{1,p} - \bar{X_1})(x_{1,p} - \bar{X_1}) & \sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{1,p} - \bar{X_1})(x_{2,p} - \bar{X_2}) & \cdots & \sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{1,p} - \bar{X_1})(x_{k,p} - \bar{X_k}) \\ \sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{2,p} - \bar{X_2})(x_{1,p} - \bar{X_1}) & \sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{2,p} - \bar{X_2})(x_{2,p} - \bar{X_2}) & \cdots & \sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{2,p} - \bar{X_2})(x_{k,p} - \bar{X_k}) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{k,p} - \bar{X_k})(x_{1,p} - \bar{X_1}) & \sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{k,p} - \bar{X_k})(x_{2,p} - \bar{X_2}) & \cdots & \sum_{p=1}^{T} (x_{k,p} - \bar{X_k})(x_{k,p} - \bar{X_k}) \end{pmatrix} \frac{1}{(n-1)}\]

Let \(\hat{\mathbf{X}}\) be the de-mean’ed \(\mathbf{X}\), or \(\hat{\mathbf{X}}=\mathbf{X}-\bar{\mathbf{X}}\). Observe that the matrix portion of [3] is equivalent to the product of the transposition of \(\hat{\mathbf{X}}\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{X}}\) self, hence:

\[\mathbf{V} = \frac{\mathbf{\hat{X}}' \mathbf{\hat{X}}}{(n-1)} = \frac{(\mathbf{X}-\mathbf{\bar{X}})'(\mathbf{X}-\mathbf{\bar{X}})}{(n-1)}\]

Furthermore, the mean of a matrix can be calculated as:

\[\mathbf{\bar{X}} = \frac{\mathbf{1} \mathbf{X}}{n}\]

where \(\mathbf{1}\) is a \(1 \times T\) row vector of \(1\)’s.

The algorithm expressed in MATLAB is as below.

load AssetReturn
trailingperiod = '30-JUL-1995::30-JUN-2015';
X = fts2mat(MonthlyReturn(trailingperiod));
[nobs, nvar] = size(X);                       % matrix sizes
X_bar = ones(1, nobs) * X / nobs;             % mean
X_hat = X - repmat(X_bar, nobs, 1);           % de-mean observations
if (nobs > 1)
    V = (X_hat' * X_hat) / (nobs - 1)
else
    V = (X_hat' * X_hat) / nobs
end
V =
    0.0172    0.0000    0.0020    0.0029    0.0023
    0.0000    0.0034    0.0018    0.0011    0.0011
    0.0020    0.0018    0.0058    0.0027    0.0025
    0.0029    0.0011    0.0027    0.0025    0.0021
    0.0023    0.0011    0.0025    0.0021    0.0019

b) Apply the matrix multiplication rule to \(\mathbf{S}\mathbf{C}\), we have [1]:

\[\mathbf{S}\mathbf{C} = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{1} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_{2} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \sigma_{k} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \rho_{1,1} & \rho_{1,2} & \cdots & \rho_{1,k} \\ \rho_{2,1} & \rho_{2,2} & \cdots & \rho_{2,k} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \rho_{k,1} & \rho_{k,2} & \cdots & \rho_{k,k} \\ \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \rho_{1,1}\sigma_{1} & \rho_{1,2}\sigma_{1} & \cdots & \rho_{1,k}\sigma_{1} \\ \rho_{2,1}\sigma_{2} & \rho_{2,2}\sigma_{2} & \cdots & \rho_{2,k}\sigma_{2} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \rho_{k,1}\sigma_{k} & \rho_{k,2}\sigma_{k} & \cdots & \rho_{k,k}\sigma_{k} \\ \end{pmatrix}\]

Again, for \((\mathbf{S}\mathbf{C})\mathbf{S}\), we have [2]:

\[(\mathbf{S}\mathbf{C})\mathbf{S} = \begin{pmatrix} \rho_{1,1}\sigma_{1} & \rho_{1,2}\sigma_{1} & \cdots & \rho_{1,k}\sigma_{1} \\ \rho_{2,1}\sigma_{2} & \rho_{2,2}\sigma_{2} & \cdots & \rho_{2,k}\sigma_{2} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \rho_{k,1}\sigma_{k} & \rho_{k,2}\sigma_{k} & \cdots & \rho_{k,k}\sigma_{k} \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{1} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_{2} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \sigma_{k} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \rho_{1,1}\sigma_{1}\sigma_{1} & \rho_{1,2}\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2} & \cdots & \rho_{1,k}\sigma_{1}\sigma_{k} \\ \rho_{2,1}\sigma_{2}\sigma_{1} & \rho_{2,2}\sigma_{2}\sigma_{1} & \cdots & \rho_{2,k}\sigma_{2}\sigma_{1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \rho_{k,1}\sigma_{k}\sigma_{1} & \rho_{k,2}\sigma_{k}\sigma_{1} & \cdots & \rho_{k,k}\sigma_{k}\sigma_{1} \\ \end{pmatrix}\]

By the definition of correlation coefficient, \(\rho_{i,j}=\frac{v_{i,j}}{\sigma{i}\sigma{j}}\). Rearrange the equation, we have \(\rho_{i,j}\sigma_{i}\sigma_{j}=v_{i,j}\). So Eq. [2] becomes:

\[\mathbf{S} \mathbf{C} \mathbf{S} = \begin{pmatrix} v_{1,1} & v_{1,2} & \cdots & v_{1,k} \\ v_{2,1} & v_{2,2} & \cdots & v_{2,k} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ v_{k,1} & v_{k,2} & \cdots & v_{k,k} \\ \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{V}\]

c) True.

Principal components of covariance matrix \(\mathbf{V}\) are uncorrelated linear combinations of the \(k\) assets that collectively explains the dependence structure of \(\mathbf{V}\). What’s more, by construction, the first principal component explains the most variances, with subsequent principal components decreasingly responsible for less variances while maintaining no correlation with preceding components.

Question 6

Again, suppose \(\mathbf{X}\) is a \(T \times k\) matrix of returns on \(k\) assets.

  1. Derive the minimum-variance portfolio.

ANSWER:

a) Problem Definition: find the optimal weights of assets in a portfolio so as to minimize portfolio risk, measured as the variance of portfolio returns, at a given level of target return.

For an \(k\)-asset portfolio, let \(\mathbf{r}\) be a \(1 \times k\) vector of the expected returns of assets, \(\mathbf{V}\) be the covariance matrix of the return series, and \(\mathbf{w}\) be a \(1 \times k\) vector of asset weights. Note that, for this problem, the historical means and covariances of returns are used as the surrogate of the expected future returns and covariances. The optimization problem can be concisely expressed as [1]:

\[\underset{\mathbf{w}}{\text{min }} v_p = \mathbf{w} \mathbf{V} \mathbf{w}'\] \[\text{s.t. } \mathbf{w} \mathbf{r}' = r_d \text{ and } \mathbf{w} \mathbf{1}' = 1\]

These constraints require that, in finding the optimal solution, a potential solution must simultaneously satisfy the conditions whereby the weights of assets must always add up to 1, as well as the portfolio return equals the target return \(r_d\).

Depending on whether short-selling is desirable, this optimization problem may be solved by different mathematical and computational solutions.

Short-selling Allowed

With short-selling allowed, Eq. [1] fully describes the optimization problem at hand. The problem is a constrained optimization problem with equality conditions that is suitably solved by Lagrange Multiplier method.

Write out the Lagrangian for Eq. [1], we have:

\[\mathbf{\Lambda}(\mathbf{w}, \lambda_1, \lambda_2) = \mathbf{w} \mathbf{V} \mathbf{w}' + \lambda_1 (\mathbf{w} \mathbf{r}' - r_d) + \lambda_2 (\mathbf{w} \mathbf{1}' - 1)\]

Finding the extrema of Eq. [1] amounts to setting the gradient of \(\mathbf{\Lambda}(\mathbf{w}, \lambda_1, \lambda_2)\) to 0 and solving the resulting system of equations [2].

\[\displaystyle\begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial \mathbf{\Lambda}(\mathbf{w}, \lambda_1, \lambda_2)} {\partial \mathbf{w}} \\ \frac{\partial \mathbf{\Lambda}(\mathbf{w}, \lambda_1, \lambda_2)} {\partial \lambda_1} \\ \frac{\partial \mathbf{\Lambda}(\mathbf{w}, \lambda_1, \lambda_2)} {\partial \lambda_2} \\ \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{0}' \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\]

With a few algebra manipulations, these equations can be conveniently simplified into a system of linear equations [3].

\[\begin{pmatrix} 2\mathbf{V} & \mathbf{1}' & \mathbf{r}' \\ \mathbf{r} & 0 & 0 \\ \mathbf{1} & 0 & 0 \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}' \\ \lambda_1 \\ \lambda_2 \\ \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{0}' \\ r_d \\ 1 \\ \end{pmatrix}\]

This is in the canonical \(\mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) form and \(\mathbf{x}\) can be solved as \(\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \mathbf{b}\). In the listing below a MATLAB solution is presented for the minimum variance problem with short-selling allowed.

%% Load data and define variables
load AssetReturn
ds = MonthlyReturn('31-JAN-2006::30-JUN-2015');
ds = fts2mat(extfield(ds, {'AAPL', 'BRKA', 'GE', 'FMAGX'}));
[~, n] = size(ds);                              % number of assets
er = mean(ds);                                  % expected returns
V = cov(ds);                                    % covariance matrix
r_d = 0.03;                                     % desired return
%% Lagrange Multiplier solution
A = [2 * V, ones(n, 1), er'; ...
     ones(1, n), 0, 0; ...
     er, 0, 0];
b = [zeros(n, 1); 1; r_d];
x = A \ b;                                      % solve Ax = b
%% Output
w = x(1:n)', pret = w * er', pstd = sqrt(w * V * w')
% Output reformatted to make it more compact
w     = 0.9871    1.0049   -0.2269   -0.7650
pret  = 0.0300
pstd  = 0.0852

NO short-selling

With short-selling prohibited, no weight of any asset can dip below 0. Coupled with the constraint that portfolio asset weights must add up to 1, it effectively means all weights must be bounded by \([0, 1]\), if short-selling were to be prohibited. Therefore one additional constraint [4] needs to be imposed on the original problem formulation in Eq. [1].

\[\mathbf{0} \le \mathbf{w} \le \mathbf{1}\]

Because of the presence of inequality constraints, Lagrange Multiplier is no longer applicable, and the problem is best solved with a numerical algorithm. Quadratic Programming turns out to be a suitable choice as the objective function Eq. [1] is indeed a quadratic function. The following is an attempt to solve this problem using MATLAB’s quadprog function from the Optimization Toolbox.

%% Load data and define variables
load AssetReturn
ds = MonthlyReturn('31-JAN-2006::30-JUN-2015');
ds = fts2mat(extfield(ds, {'AAPL', 'BRKA', 'GE', 'FMAGX'}));
[~, n] = size(ds);                              % number of assets
er = mean(ds);                                  % expected returns
V = cov(ds);                                    % covariance matrix
r_d = 0.02;                                     % desired return
Aeq = ones(1, n); beq = 1;                      % equality Aeq*x = beq
Aineq = -er; bineq = -r_d;                      % inequality Aineq*x <= bineq
lb = zeros(n, 1); ub = ones(n, 1);              % bounds lb <= x <= ub
c = zeros(n, 1);                                % objective has no linear term; 
                                                % set it to zero
%% Select the interior point algorithm in quadprog
options = optimoptions('quadprog', 'Algorithm', 'interior-point-convex');
%% Quadratic Programming solution
x = quadprog(V, c, Aineq, bineq, Aeq, beq, lb, ub, [], options);
%% Output
w = x', pret = w * er', pstd = sqrt(w * V * w')
% MATLAB output
w     = 0.6041    0.3959    0.0000    0.0000
pret  = 0.0020
pstd  = 0.0636

Question 7

Given \(\mathbf{V}\) and a weight vector \(\mathbf{w}\) (\(1 \times k\)), derive:

  1. The marginal contribution to risk (this is a \(k \times 1\) vector) and explain it.
  2. Derive the risk attribution (a \(k \times 1\) vector whose sum is the portfolio risk).

ANSWER:

a) The marginal contribution to risk (MCTR thereafter) is the sensitivity of the risk of a portfolio to a change in the amount of the underlying holdings. Semantically, it is the increase/decrease in the portfolio risk caused by holding one unit of an underlying holding. Mathematically, MCTR is the partial derivative of the portfolio risk with regard to the holding weights.

Let the portfolio risk \(\sigma_p = \sqrt{\mathbf{w} \mathbf{V} \mathbf{w}'}\). Denote the MCTR as \(\mathbf{c_M}\), a \(k \times 1\) vector. The MCTR can be expressed as follows.

\[\mathbf{c_M} = \frac{\partial \sigma_p}{\partial \mathbf{w}} = \frac{\partial \sqrt{\mathbf{w} \mathbf{V} \mathbf{w}'}}{\partial \mathbf{w}}= \frac{1}{2 \sigma_p} \frac{\partial \mathbf{w} \mathbf{V} \mathbf{w}'}{\partial \mathbf{w}} = \frac{\mathbf{V} \mathbf{w}'}{\sigma_p}\]

b) Given a MCTR \(\mathbf{c_M}\), the absolute risk attribution \(\mathbf{c_A}\) of holdings can be calculated as:

\[\mathbf{c_A} = \mathbf{c_M} \operatorname{diag}(\mathbf{w})\]

where the operator ‘\(\operatorname{diag}\)’ converts a row/column vector into a diagonal matrix. The result is a \(k \times 1\) column vector, with elements summing up to the portfolio risk \(\sigma_p\), or \(\sum \mathbf{c_A} = \sigma_p\).

Question 9

We have, again, a \(T \times k\) matrix of returns \(\mathbf{X}\). In Matlab, write the code that will solve for the maximum Sharpe ratio portfolio with adding up constraint (sum of \(w\) = 1), no shorting, and an upper bound on the \(k\) th asset weight equal to 0.1.

ANSWER:

As no risk-free rate is given, here we calculate the Sharpe ratio without adjusting for the risk-free rate. This omission hardly matters anyway in light of the current zero intereat rate environment. The optimization problem can be succinctly expressed as [1]:

\[\underset{\mathbf{w}}{\text{max }} sr_p = \frac{\mathbf{w} \mathbf{r}'}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w} \mathbf{V} \mathbf{w}'}}\] \[\text{s.t. } \mathbf{w} \mathbf{1}' = 1\] \[\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \le \begin{pmatrix} w_1 \\ w_2 \\ \vdots \\ w_{k-1} \\ w_k \end{pmatrix} \le \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \\ 0.1 \end{pmatrix}\]

Before getting into the main routine, let us get the explanations of a few ancillary functions out of the way.

Function estimateEfficientPortfolio

This function solves the efficient (minimum variance) portfolio at a given level of target return. It is simply an encapsulation of the same algorithms developed as the solution to Question 6. (Source listing omitted due to significant overlap with Question 6).

Function estimateGlobalMinVar

A function that estimates the Global Minimal Variance portfolio. The return on the GMV portfolio establishes the lower bound of the range of returns over which the local optimization of Sharpe ratio is conducted. The following outlines the optimization problem for the GMV portfolio as well as the MATLAB implementation.

\[\underset{\mathbf{w}}{\text{min }} v_p = \mathbf{w} \mathbf{V} \mathbf{w}'\] \[\text{ s.t. } \mathbf{w} \mathbf{1}' = 1\]

function wgt = globalMinVar(V, er, lb, ub)
%GLOBALMINVAR global minimum variance portfolio

n = numel(er);                                  % number of assets
Aeq = ones(1, n); beq = 1;                      % equality Aeq*x = beq
c = zeros(n, 1);                                % objective has no linear term;
                                                % set it to zero
% Quadratic Programming solution
optquadprog = optimoptions('quadprog', 'Algorithm', 'interior-point-convex', ...
                           'Display', 'off');
wgt = quadprog(V, c, [], [], Aeq, beq, lb, ub, [], optquadprog);
end

Function estimateSharpeRatioNeg

The objective function for the local optimization problem of finding the maximum Sharpe ratio portfolio over a range of possible returns. The result is negated so that the function can be used in conjunction with the fminbnd built-in function to search for the maximum return.

function sharpe = estimateSharpRatioNeg(V, er, rd, lb, ub)
%ESTIMATESHARPERATIONEG estimate Sharpe ratio of an efficient portfolio that
%yields the target return.

wgt = estimateEfficientPortfolio(V, er, rd, lb, ub);
r = wgt' * er'; s = sqrt(wgt' * V * wgt);
sharpe = -(r / s);
end

Main Script

First, the monthly return of candidate assets are loaded, and variables are defined.

%% load data and define variables
load AssetReturn
mr = MonthlyReturn('31-JAN-2006::30-JUN-2015');
mr = fts2mat(extfield(mr, {'AAPL', 'BRKA', 'GE', 'FMAGX'}));
[~, n] = size(mr);                     % number of assets
er = mean(mr);                         % expected returns
V = cov(mr);                           % covariance matrix
lb = zeros(n, 1); ub = ones(n, 1);     % bounds lb <= x <= ub
ub(n, 1) = 0.1;                        % weight of the nth asset w_n <= 0.1

Second, the lower and upper bounds of potential portfolio returns are established.

  • Lower bound: expected return achieved by the Global Minimum Variance portfolio
  • Upper bound: the best return among all candidate assets. Without short-selling and leverage, the best return a portfolio can ever achieve is if the entire budget is invested in the highest returning asset.
%% find the global minimum variance portfolio
gmwgt = globalMinVar(V, er, lb, ub);
%% range of possible returns over which the max Sharpe portfolio is sought
minret = gmwgt' * er'; maxret = max(er);
minret: 1.10%, maxret = 2.75%

Next, the maximum Sharpe ratio is sought through a bisection method implemented in the MATLAB built-in function fminbnd. The end product of this step is the return expected of the maximum Sharpe ratio portfolio.

%% bounded search of the max Sharpe ratio portfolio
fobj = @(rd) estimateSharpeRatioNeg(V, er, rd, lb, ub);
optfminbnd = optimset('fminbnd');
optfminbnd = optimset(optfminbnd, 'Display', 'on', 'TolX', 1.0e-8);
r = fminbnd(fobj, minret, maxret, optfminbnd);

Finally, the asset weights of the max Sharpe ratio portfolio are solved, portfolio risk and Sharpe ratio are calculated, and results printed to console.

%% solve portfolio weights of the max Sharpe ratio portfolio
pwgt = estimateEfficientPortfolio(V, er, r, lb, ub);
%% output
sd = sqrt(pwgt' * V * pwgt); sharpe = r / sd;

Question 10

Suppose \(\mathbf{Z}\) is a \(T \times 6\) matrix of returns on three assets (columns 1-3) and three factors (columns 4-6). Use the covariance of \(\mathbf{Z}\) to derive betas on the three factors (for each asset).

ANSWER:

\(\beta\) is commonly expressed as

\[\beta = \frac{Cov(r_a,r_f)}{Var(r_f)}\]

where \(r_a\) is the asset return and \(r_f\) is the factor return, the operator \(Cov(X, Y)\) is the covariance between RV \(X\) and \(Y\), and \(Var(X)\) is the variance of RV \(X\).

We are given a covariance matrix \(\mathbf{V}\) of the matrix of returns \(\mathbf{Z}\).

Let the three assets be denoted as \((a,b,c)\), and factors be \((x,y,z)\), the covariance matrix \(\mathbf{V}\) is written in full as:

\[\mathbf{V} = \begin{pmatrix} v_{a,a} & v_{a,b} & v_{a,c} & v_{a,x} & v_{a,y} & v_{a,z} \\ v_{b,a} & v_{b,b} & v_{b,c} & v_{b,x} & v_{b,y} & v_{b,z} \\ v_{c,a} & v_{c,b} & v_{c,c} & v_{c,x} & v_{c,y} & v_{c,z} \\ v_{x,a} & v_{x,b} & v_{x,c} & v_{x,x} & v_{x,y} & v_{x,z} \\ v_{y,a} & v_{y,b} & v_{y,c} & v_{y,x} & v_{y,y} & v_{y,z} \\ v_{z,a} & v_{z,b} & v_{z,c} & v_{z,x} & v_{z,y} & v_{z,z} \\ \end{pmatrix} \Rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} \Box & \Box & \Box & v_{a,x} & v_{a,y} & v_{a,z} \\ \Box & \Box & \Box & v_{b,x} & v_{b,y} & v_{b,z} \\ \Box & \Box & \Box & v_{c,x} & v_{c,y} & v_{c,z} \\ \Box & \Box & \Box & v_{x,x} & \Box & \Box \\ \Box & \Box & \Box & \Box & v_{y,y} & \Box \\ \Box & \Box & \Box & \Box & \Box & v_{z,z} \\ \end{pmatrix}\]

Denote the \(3 \times 3\) submatrix from the top-right corner of \(\mathbf{V}\) as \(\mathbf{C}\), a matrix of covariances between the assets and factors, the \(3 \times 3\) submatrix from the bottom-right corner of \(\mathbf{V}\) as \(\mathbf{D}\), the diagonal of \(\mathbf{D}\) being the variances of the factors. The \(\beta\)’s for each asset can be calculated as follows:

\[\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{C} \cdot \operatorname{diag}^{-1}(\mathbf{D}) = \begin{pmatrix} v_{a,x} & v_{a,y} & v_{a,z} \\ v_{b,x} & v_{b,y} & v_{b,z} \\ v_{c,x} & v_{c,y} & v_{c,z} \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{v_{x,x}} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{v_{y,y}} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{v_{z,z}} \\ \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{v_{a,x}}{v_{x,x}}&\frac{v_{a,y}}{v_{y,y}}&\frac{v_{a,z}}{v_{z,z}}\\ \frac{v_{b,x}}{v_{x,x}}&\frac{v_{b,y}}{v_{y,y}}&\frac{v_{b,z}}{v_{z,z}}\\ \frac{v_{c,x}}{v_{x,x}}&\frac{v_{c,y}}{v_{y,y}}&\frac{v_{c,z}}{v_{z,z}}\\ \end{pmatrix}\]

The column vectors of \(\mathbf{B}\) are the beta coeffients for assets \((a,b,c)\) respectively.
